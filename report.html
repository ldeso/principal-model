Phase B — Fee vs. Principal Simulator

Interactive companion to the research note

What this report is about (for new readers)

Imagine a company that helps businesses retire tonnes of carbon-offset credits on a blockchain protocol (here, Klima). To retire one tonne, the company must first buy the protocol’s token on the open market, and the token’s dollar price moves around every day. The company has to decide how to charge customers and how to source inventory, and those two choices together determine how much money it makes and how much risk it runs.

This notebook compares two business designs for that company:

  • Fee-based. The company quotes the customer a markup on top of the live token price and buys just-in-time. It never holds a big inventory and it never takes a directional bet, but its revenue rises and falls with the token price.
  • Principal. The company quotes the customer a fixed dollar price per tonne today, locking in the sale — and then takes on the job of finding the tokens. That can be done by (3a) buying all the tokens up front, (3b) buying them one-by-one as retirements happen, or (3c) any blend of the two.

The fee model behaves like a commission-taker. The principal model behaves like a market-maker that has shorted a stream of forwards on the token: it locked in a sale price and now has to cover it at an unknown cost. The key question for the business is: given how the token price is expected to move, which model makes more money, which one loses more in a bad scenario, and how much does pre-buying inventory (the “hedge ratio” α) reduce that risk?

The companion research note (research-note.md) derives the math. This notebook runs the numbers: it simulates thousands of possible futures for the token price, computes the company’s profit and loss (P&L) in each one, and plots the distributions.

Minimum background to read this page. Familiarity with probability (mean, standard deviation, quantiles) and with Monte Carlo simulation helps, but the prose in each section explains what every plot is showing and what a “good” or “bad” pattern looks like. Readers who want the derivations should keep research-note.md open alongside; readers who just want the business intuition can skim past the equations.

Overview

This notebook is the Phase B companion to research-note.md (Phase A, the mathematical note). Phase A wrote down closed-form formulas for the mean and variance of every book’s P&L under a standard price model (Geometric Brownian Motion, or GBM — see the next section). Phase B, this notebook, has three jobs:

  1. Cross-check the math. For quantities the Phase A note gives in closed form, we also compute them by Monte Carlo simulation. If the two disagree, one of them is wrong. The z column in the §4 table is a numerical sanity check built exactly for this purpose.
  2. Fill in what closed form cannot give. Tail-risk metrics (VaR, CVaR, loss probability — all defined below) depend on the full distribution, not just its first two moments. We estimate those empirically.
  3. Let the reader explore. The (α, μ, σ) grid at the bottom of the page is interactive: drag the sliders to see how the tail of the principal book responds to drift, volatility, and the hedge ratio.

All Monte Carlo computation happens in TypeScript (src/); this page only reads the emitted JSON and visualises it. If you are running the report locally, regenerate the data before previewing:

npm run sweep -- --seed 42

What you will see below. Four kinds of plots: (1) a scorecard table of closed-form vs Monte Carlo P&L moments with tail metrics, (2) overlaid P&L distributions for the three stochastic models, (3) diagnostics on the shared random kernel I_T := ∫₀ᵀ S_t dt (the time-integral of the simulated price; both the fee and back-to-back books turn out to be linear functions of this single quantity, so it is worth looking at directly), and (4) parameter sweeps showing how tail risk and the break-even quote Q* respond to drift μ, volatility σ, and hedge ratio α.

Mini-glossary

If you are coming from outside quant finance, these three concepts come up repeatedly on this page:

  • Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM). A standard model for prices that evolve by random percentage moves (not random dollar moves). Two parameters: μ is the expected growth rate per year (“drift”), σ is the size of the random jitter per year (“volatility”). This is the same model underneath Black–Scholes.
  • Monte Carlo simulation. Rolling the dice many thousands of times. Here each “roll” is a full random trajectory of the token price over the horizon T, and we compute the company’s P&L along that trajectory. Averaging over thousands of trajectories gives us an estimate of expected P&L; looking at the worst outcomes gives us tail risk.
  • Hedge ratio α. The fraction of the tokens the company pre-buys at day zero instead of buying just-in-time. α = 0 means no pre-buying at all (maximum exposure to price); α = 1 means buying the whole year’s supply at inception (fully pre-hedged, zero terminal risk); α = 0.5 means half now, half later.

Notation cheat-sheet

Quick reading guide for the less-obvious symbols:

  • P is a protocol constant: how many tokens the protocol wants per tonne of CO₂ retired. It doesn’t move. The dollar price per tonne π_t = P·S_t moves only because the token price S_t moves.
  • I_T is the area under the price curve over the horizon — if you sketched S_t against time for t ∈ [0,T], I_T is the area under that sketch. It shows up repeatedly because integrating the price over a period of time is exactly what “pay the running market rate for a steady flow of tonnes” costs.
  • R_fee, Π_b2b, Π_matched, Π_α are the four different P&L variables compared in the §4 table below. R_fee is revenue (always ≥ 0); the Π’s are profit-and-loss (can be negative).
  • Q* is the “fair” per-tonne quote for the principal model — the price at which its expected P&L matches the fee model’s. The last section of this report plots how Q* depends on μ and T.

§4 — P&L moments: closed-form vs Monte Carlo

This is the central scorecard. Each row is one of the four books from the research note (the fee book, and three principal variants: fully matched 3a, fully back-to-back 3b, and partially hedged 3c), and each column is a summary statistic of that book’s P&L distribution.

The table has two logical halves:

  1. Sanity check (first block of columns). For quantities the Phase A note derives in closed form — expected P&L and its standard deviation — we print both the closed-form value and the Monte Carlo estimate. If our simulator is correct, these two should agree. The z column formalises that: it is the MC mean’s distance from the closed form, measured in standard errors of the MC mean. Rule of thumb: |z| ≤ 2 looks healthy, |z| > 3 is worth investigating.
  2. Risk metrics (second block). Quantities that can’t be computed from the first two moments alone: VaR, CVaR, loss probability, and a Sharpe-like ratio. These come straight from the empirical distribution of simulated P&Ls.

All P&L numbers are in USD over the horizon T (not annualised, not per-tonne).

column meaning
E[Π] (cf) / (mc) expected P&L — closed form vs Monte Carlo
±CI95 ±95% half-width of the MC mean
SD (cf) / (mc) standard deviation of P&L
z (MC−cf) / stderr; MC sanity check
VaR95 / VaR99 loss threshold exceeded with 5% / 1% probability
CVaR95 / CVaR99 expected loss in the worst 5% / 1% tail
P[Π<0] probability of a losing realisation
Sharpe E[Π] / SD[Π], invariant under f-scaling for fee model

Risk metrics in plain English.

  • VaR₉₅ (Value-at-Risk at 95%). A loss number. “With 5% probability we lose at least VaR₉₅.” It’s the 5th-percentile of P&L, sign-flipped. A common executive-summary number, but it deliberately does not tell you how bad the worst 5% are — only the threshold at which that 5% begins.
  • CVaR₉₅ (Conditional VaR, also “Expected Shortfall”). The average loss inside that worst 5%. If VaR₉₅ is the door to the tail, CVaR₉₅ is the average depth of the room beyond. CVaR is a strictly more informative risk measure than VaR when the tail is fat or skewed.
  • VaR₉₉ / CVaR₉₉. Same, but the worst 1% rather than the worst 5%. Use these for rarer but more severe events.
  • P[Π < 0]. The probability the book simply loses money at the horizon. A blunt headline metric.
  • Sharpe-like = E[Π] / SD[Π]. Expected reward per unit of risk — higher is better. For the fee model this number is invariant in f (doubling the fee doubles both mean and SD), so it captures structural risk/reward, not the pricing dial.

Reading tips.

  • Row fee: revenue only (never negative), so VaR/CVaR are negative numbers, i.e. “worst case is still a gain, just a smaller one.”
  • Row principal_3a: by construction the matched book has no terminal randomness at all, so SD, CI95, and z are zero to machine precision, and VaR/CVaR simply echo the sign of the one deterministic P&L.
  • Row principal_3b: the unhedged principal book. This is the row where the loss tail is real — compare its CVaR₉₉ against the others to see how much a bad price path costs.
  • Row principal_3c: the partially-hedged book. Expect its SD and CVaR to be smaller than 3b’s by roughly (1−α)² — the variance formula from Phase A §3c.

P&L distributions

The table above summarises each book with a handful of statistics. This plot shows the whole distribution behind those statistics — one panel per stochastic book. The shapes tell the same story the numbers told, but visually.

How to read a panel:

  • The horizontal axis is the book’s terminal P&L in USD over the horizon T. Negative values are losses.
  • The bars are a histogram of simulated P&L outcomes, rescaled as an empirical density.
  • The solid black vertical rule sits at the closed-form expected P&L E[Π]. If Monte Carlo is converged, the visual centre of the histogram should land right on it.
  • The dashed grey vertical rule sits at Π = 0 — the break-even line. Whatever mass of the histogram sits to the left of it is the loss probability P[Π<0] reported in the table.

What to notice on each panel:

  • Fee book (R_fee = f·P·λ·I_T). Pure revenue: the formula has no minus sign, so this random variable is non-negative by construction. The histogram sits entirely in the right half-plane (no mass to the left of Π = 0). Its shape is right-skewed — a long right tail — because it inherits the right-skew of I_T (see next section).
  • Principal 3b (back-to-back, α = 0). Same random kernel I_T, but it enters with a minus sign, so the distribution is the mirror of the fee book’s: upside is capped (best case: token price collapses to zero, company gets the full fixed payment Q·N), downside is unbounded (worst case: token rallies, company has to buy expensively to fill the contract). This left-skewed loss tail is the central risk concern of the whole research project.
  • Principal 3c (partial, α = 0.5 in this run). Same qualitative shape as 3b, but narrower by a factor of (1−α)² = 0.25 on variance, i.e. (1−α) = 0.5 on standard deviation. This is the visual demonstration that pre-buying tokens shrinks the loss tail.

I_T histogram vs. Dufresne closed-form moments

I_T := ∫₀ᵀ S_t dt is the time-integral of the simulated price: the area under the price-vs-time curve over the horizon. Why does this one random number deserve its own plot?

Because every book’s P&L turns out to be a simple linear function of I_T. The fee book’s revenue is f·P·λ·I_T. The back-to-back principal book’s P&L is Q·N − P·λ·I_T. The partially-hedged book is an affine combination of those two. So I_T is the one random variable that drives everything stochastic in this notebook; every other distribution is just a rescaling or reflection of this one.

Dufresne (2001) gives closed-form formulas for the mean and variance of I_T, but not for its full distribution — I_T is not log-normally distributed, even though S_t is, so tail quantiles have to be estimated by Monte Carlo. This plot is that estimate:

  • Histogram bars are the simulated density of I_T across all paths.
  • Solid black rule is the Dufresne closed-form mean E[I_T] = S_0·(e^{μT}−1)/μ. If the simulator is correct, it should land on the visual centre of the histogram.
  • Dashed black rules are E[I_T] ± SD[I_T] — roughly a one-sigma band. A well-behaved histogram puts ~68% of its mass between them.
itHist = {
  const { edges, counts } = run.itSampleHistogram;
  const total = counts.reduce((a,b) => a+b, 0);
  return counts.map((c,i) => ({
    x0: edges[i], x1: edges[i+1], density: c / (total * (edges[i+1]-edges[i]))
  }));
}

Sample GBM paths — matched-book NAV view (§3a)

The matched pre-purchase book (α = 1) has a deterministic P&L at the horizon — the operator locked in a sale price Q per tonne and also locked in a purchase cost P·S_0 per tonne at day zero, so the final number doesn’t depend on the random path S_t at all.

So why worry? Because in the meantime the business is carrying the token inventory on its balance sheet, and that inventory’s mark-to- market value moves with the token price. If the token price tanks in month three, the accounting value of the remaining tokens falls — and that can trigger margin calls, covenant breaches, or simply painful quarterly reporting, even if by the horizon the book is mathematically fine.

The Phase A note defines this mark-to-market inventory value as

V_t = (N · P) · (1 − t/T) · S_t.

Read the formula in parts:

  • N · P is the total number of tokens originally bought.
  • (1 − t/T) is the fraction still in inventory at time t — it starts at 1 (all of them) and decays linearly to 0 at the horizon, because retirements burn them at the constant rate λ.
  • S_t is the current dollar price per token.

So V_t = (tokens still held) × (current price per token) — ordinary mark-to-market. At t = 0 it equals V_0 = N·P·S_0, the original purchase cost. At t = T it equals zero, because the last token has been retired and there is no inventory left to value.

The plot shows a handful of sampled paths of V_t:

  • Grey lines are random trajectories — each one is a possible future history of the NAV.
  • Orange line is the first path, highlighted so one trajectory is easy to follow with the eye.
  • Notice that every path pins to zero at t = T regardless of whether the token price went up or down. That is the burn schedule doing its work.
  • The scary bit is the dip in the middle. Even a path that recovers to a reasonable terminal NAV can touch a painful low partway through. The next plot quantifies that.

NAV shortfall vs. schedule (§3a)

For the matched book, the relevant solvency question is: “If the token price had stayed flat at S_0 the whole time, the NAV would have decayed along a straight line from N·P·S_0 down to zero. How far below that straight line did the actual NAV dip, in the worst case?”

Phrased as a formula, that is

D := max_{t ≤ T}  [ N·P·(1 − t/T) · (S_0 − S_t) ]₊

where [x]₊ means max(x, 0) — we only count the part where the actual NAV is below the no-price-move schedule, not above it (an upward excursion is a paper gain, not a solvency risk).

This histogram is the empirical distribution of that maximum shortfall D across all simulated paths:

  • The x-axis is dollars of shortfall relative to the flat-price schedule. Further right = deeper dip = worse.
  • The dashed black rule is the 95th percentile — a number that, on average, will be exceeded on 1 out of every 20 years simulated.
  • The solid black rule is the 99th percentile — exceeded on 1 out of every 100. These two percentiles are the natural stress thresholds the treasury team would want to size its capital buffer against.

Even though the matched book has deterministic terminal P&L, this plot shows why it is not “risk-free” in any practical sense: a large interim drawdown is a real operational event.

Tail risk vs volatility σ (fixed α, μ)

Holding α (the hedge ratio) and μ (the drift) fixed at the values chosen above, this line shows how the CVaR₉₅ of the 3c book grows as we crank up σ (volatility).

Intuition. Volatility is the size of the random jitter in the token price. More jitter means the integral I_T is more spread out, which means the principal book’s P&L is more spread out, which means its left tail is fatter — so the average loss inside that tail is bigger. We therefore expect the curve below to be monotonically increasing (higher volatility → worse tail loss). That’s a useful sanity check: if the line is non-monotone or flat, something is wrong upstream.

Tail risk vs hedge ratio α (fixed μ, σ at grid[1])

This is the plot that answers the business question “how much do I reduce my loss tail by pre-buying more tokens up front?”

It fixes μ at the selected value and σ at the second value in the grid (a middle-of-the-road volatility so the shape is easy to read), then sweeps α from 0 (fully back-to-back, no pre-buying) to 1 (fully matched, entire year’s supply bought today).

Phase A §3c showed that Var[Π_α] = (1 − α)² · Var[Π_b2b]. Since CVaR scales roughly with standard deviation in a broad family of distributions, the CVaR₉₅ curve should collapse quadratically toward zero as α → 1. Visually: expect a smooth convex curve that bends down steeply, not a straight line. That’s the signature of the (1 − α)² factor.

At α = 1 the book is fully pre-hedged and terminal P&L is deterministic; at that point the CVaR₉₅ drops to the sign of the one deterministic P&L (a single value, not a tail).

This plot is the visual answer to: “Pre-buying tokens is the natural hedge for the back-to-back principal book.” The curve below is the dose-response curve for that hedge.

Break-even quote surface Q*(μ, T)

This section answers the pricing question: “If I were going to switch from the fee model to the principal model, what per-tonne quote Q would I need to charge so that my expected revenue is the same?” The answer is Q*, and it depends on how the operator expects the token price to drift (μ) and how long the contract runs (T).

Q* is the USD quote that equalises the expected P&L of the back-to-back principal book with the fee-based book:

Q* = (1 + f) · P · S_0 · (e^{μT} − 1) / (μ T).

To make the picture easier to read, we divide both sides by (1+f)·P·S_0 — this removes the uninteresting scaling (which is just today’s fee-quote per tonne) and leaves behind the interesting shape:

Q* / ((1+f)·P·S_0)  =  (e^{μT} − 1) / (μT).

The ratio on the right is dimensionless — it is the factor by which the principal quote has to exceed the fee quote to compensate for expected price drift. A value of 1.0 means “quote the same as the fee model”; a value above 1.0 means “charge a premium to cover expected token appreciation”; a value below 1.0 means “can afford to undercut the fee model because the token is expected to cheapen.”

On the plot:

  • Each coloured line is one value of drift μ from the grid (see legend). Colour goes from low-to-high drift via the viridis ramp.
  • The dashed horizontal rule at 1.0 is the μ = 0 limit: if the token price is expected to stay flat, the principal operator should quote the fee quote (1+f)·P·S_0 and the ratio is exactly 1.
  • Lines for μ > 0 sit above the dashed line (principal model must charge more to cover expected appreciation).
  • Lines for μ < 0 sit below the dashed line (principal model can charge less because it expects the token to cheapen, saving on inventory costs).
  • The longer the horizon T, the more compound growth piles up, and the further the line moves away from 1. Short horizons barely depart from the dashed line at all.

Reading notes

A few things to keep in mind while skimming or coming back to this report later.

On the numbers you see:

  • All closed-form / Monte Carlo agreement checks live in the z column of the §4 table — a single glance should tell you whether the MC is well-converged at the current nPaths. Consistently large |z| values are the first thing to investigate; they almost always point to either a bug in the simulator or too few paths for the statistic you’re reading.
  • The fee and back-to-back books share the same random kernel I_T, so their variance ratio is the exact algebraic multiple (P/f)². If you ever read a ratio that disagrees with that constant, you are looking at either a simulator bug or a distribution other than the one this report claims to be showing.
  • All P&L numbers are totals over the horizon T, not annualised and not per-tonne. If you want a per-tonne number, divide by N = λ·T.
  • “Loss” throughout means “the P&L random variable Π came out negative.” The fee book’s P&L variable R_fee is really a revenue, so it is never negative — its VaR/CVaR as printed are the low end of revenue, not a loss against zero.

On the business take-aways:

  • The fee book has revenue that is always ≥ 0 but strictly dependent on token price: when the token is cheap, revenue is small. No catastrophe, but no upside beyond the fee markup either.
  • The unhedged principal book (3b) has a capped upside and an unbounded downside. It is structurally short the token. The more the operator believes the token will rally, the worse this model looks — and the break-even quote Q* plot above makes that explicit.
  • Pre-buying tokens (moving α from 0 toward 1) is the natural hedge for the principal book: variance shrinks as (1 − α)², so even a modest α buys a disproportionate reduction in tail risk. But pre-buying ties up capital (α · N · P · S_0) and introduces interim NAV drawdown risk — quantified in the §3a plots.
  • At Q = Q*, the fee book and the back-to-back principal book have equal expected P&L but very different distributions. The principal book trades its mean preservation for a left-skewed loss tail. This asymmetry is the single most important thing the report is trying to show.

On what this notebook does not do:

  • This notebook is Phase B. Phase C extensions (jump diffusion, Poisson demand, historical calibration against a real kVCM proxy, dynamic hedging strategies) are intentionally out of scope here. The current numbers assume idealised GBM price dynamics and a deterministic retirement flow; those assumptions are the cleanest ones under which closed-form results exist and can be cross-checked.
  • Gas, slippage, and discounting are not modelled in the baseline. They are parameterizable hooks in the simulator (see src/params.ts) but are zeroed out in this report.
  • Nothing here is financial advice or a calibration against any real token. Use the framework; plug in your own (μ, σ) calibrated from data before making any business decision.
